Pre-breeding: Transforming Biodiversity into Innovation for Future Crops

Bridging genetic resources with commercial breeding for resilient agriculture

In recent years, my academic and personal journey through the horticultural world has often led me to reflect on a seemingly simple question: where does innovation truly come from in crop genetic improvement?

The answer I've found doesn't concern just new technologies or the increasingly sophisticated molecular tools that I have witnessed during the many excursions to breeding companies, but something deeper and more structural: the ability to identify, evaluate, and make usable plant biodiversity, even when it comes from materials that are poorly or not at all adapted.

During my Master's in Plant Sciences at Wageningen University & Research, and as I gradually approached the world of applied research and seed production, I understood how pre-breeding represents a key element of this process. An essential bridge, often not very visible, between genebanks, basic research, and commercial breeding programs, but crucial for crop resilience in the long term.

One of the most valuable aspects of this academic journey was the opportunity to gain insight into all these interconnected activities and to learn how to connect different perspectives. I still vividly remember my visit to the Centre for Genetic Resources in Wageningen as part of the Seed science and technology class, a remarkable heritage of more than 24.000 varieties and wild populations of food crops from all over the world, carefully conserved, characterized and made available to anyone for research.

Visit at the Centre for Genetic Resources at Wageningen University and Research

Equally inspiring were the many conversations with plant breeders from major breeding companies in the Netherlands and Germany. From these discussions I truly realized how the development of new varieties is driven not only by emerging biotic and abiotic stress, but also by the needs of those who stand at the end of the production chain, from people responsible for harvesting and processing to, ultimately, consumers themselves.

Why pre-breeding is strategic today

Genetic improvement is not a linear process. Rather, it is a continuous flow of genetic recombination, selection, and testing that can be suddenly disrupted by the emergence of new biotic pressures, climate-related challenges, or changing market demands.

Through conversations with breeders with many years of experience, one idea has emerged very clearly: years of work focused on optimizing yield, quality, and uniformity can be quickly set aside when a new disease appears, forcing breeding programs to restart from resistant genetic sources that are often initially less competitive from an agronomic perspective.

The introduction of resistance to emerging viruses such as Tomato brown rugose fruit virus (ToBRFV) is a clear example of this dynamic. Today, this pathogen represents a major threat in Dutch glasshouses, requiring strict hygiene protocols and reshaping breeding priorities. In such situations, genetic resistance becomes the primary goal, while productivity and quality are gradually rebuilt through successive cycles of selection and testing.

For this reason, I believe that exploring wild accessions as sources of novel resistance traits is a powerful approach to developing more resilient food crops and strengthening global food security. At the same time, preserving this broad diversity of wild genetic resources is essential to ensure that this variability is not irreversibly lost.

Although my academic background is not rooted in classical breeding but in plant physiology, one of the aspects that fascinates me most is the direct interaction between genetics, environment, and cultivation practices. I still remember one of my first classes in plant physiology in horticulture, when we discussed what a chimera is, how it forms, and how stable it can be: a perfect example of how mutations in specific meristem layers can give rise to beautifully variegated plants, which may occasionally revert their color in order to restore higher chlorophyll content.

Example of variegated Hosta (left) and Actinidia (right) from the Arboretum de Dreijen in Wageningen.

During my internship as a Research Intern at the Delphy Improvement Centre in the Netherlands, I followed several horticultural crops throughout their entire growth cycle, from trial design and crop monitoring to harvest and data analysis. This experience highlighted several key points:

  • The same genotype can express very different phenotypes depending on the environment
  • Complex traits such as yield, vigor, and stress tolerance cannot be evaluated in isolation
  • Repeated testing across different environments remains indispensable, even when working with highly homozygous or double-haploid lines
Dutch tomato greenhouse.

These observations reinforced my view that rigorous testing is central to pre-breeding, as is collaboration across disciplines. This perspective was further strengthened by joining the course on pre-breeding offered by the Food and Agriculture Organization, which emphasized that the success of a program relies on:

  • Effective cooperation between genebank managers and breeders
  • Accurate germplasm characterization, both phenotypic and, when possible, molecular
  • A long-term vision that often extends beyond short-term objectives

Looking ahead

Working in the seed sector means recognizing the seed as a strategic unit. Genetic purity, health, germinability, and stability are not secondary aspects, but essential conditions for grower confidence and for the sustainability of the entire production chain.

Looking ahead, I am interested in contributing to applied research and pre-breeding contexts where plant physiology, chemistry, and genetics come together. Understanding how plants function, respond to stress, and express genetic potential is, in my view, a necessary step toward making better use of existing biodiversity and translating it into solutions that support resilient cropping systems and, ultimately, global food security.

My academic path has been shaped by diverse perspectives, which has influenced the way I approach plant sciences: with curiosity, attention to biological complexity, and an interest in connecting different disciplines. I see these elements not as a destination, but as tools to keep learning and to contribute, step by step, to the development of more robust and sustainable agricultural systems.
